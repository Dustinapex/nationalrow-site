// The situation

A condemnation notice is overwhelming by design.

The agency arrives with its own appraisal, its own number, and its own timeline — for what may be one of the largest assets you own. The process is unfamiliar and built around their interests, not yours.

  • 01A highway department, utility, or pipeline company is taking part of your land or putting an easement across it.
  • 02Their offer arrives with an appraisal you didn't choose — and no easy way to know if it's fair.
  • 03Only a strip is taken, but it damages your access, your frontage, or the use of everything that's left.
  • 04The paperwork and deadlines feel designed to make you sign quickly and move on.

The direct answer: you can contest the value — and you don't have to face the agency alone.

You may not be able to stop the project — but you can make sure you're paid in full for it. National ROW takes the process off your shoulders, builds the case for what your property is truly worth, and stands between you and the agency from the first call to the final number.

Guide

Do I have to accept the condemnation offer?

No. The agency's first offer is an opening position, not a take-it-or-leave-it number — and you generally have the right to question it and pursue more.

Why the first offer is rarely the real number

The condemning agency, utility, or pipeline builds its offer from its own appraisal, prepared on its own assumptions and timeline. That number is designed to get the project moving, and it commonly leaves money on the table — especially for the damage done to the land you keep.

What you can do instead of signing

Get an independent read on value before you respond. Make sure the claim reflects the whole picture: the land or easement taken, the damage to the remainder, lost or changed access, and any impact to buildings, crops, or operations. Then negotiate from facts rather than from the agency's first figure.

What it doesn't mean

Questioning the value usually won't stop the project. But it can change what you are paid for it — sometimes substantially. The goal isn't to fight progress; it's to be paid in full for your share of it.

Guide

What are "damages to the remainder"?

When only part of your property is taken, "damages to the remainder" is the lost value of the land you keep — and it is often the largest part of a fair claim.

What causes remainder damage

Common causes include lost or rerouted access, an odd leftover shape, a strip that splits the parcel in two, reduced usable acreage, and being placed right next to a new highway, tower line, or pipeline. Each can lower the value of everything you still own.

How it is measured

Appraisers typically use a before-and-after approach: the value of the whole property before the taking compared to the value of what remains afterward. The difference — beyond the strip itself — can be compensable.

Why it gets undercounted

An agency's offer may value only the dirt inside the take and quietly skip what happens to the rest. That gap is exactly where owners most often recover more.

Guide

Easement vs. fee taking: what's the difference?

A fee taking transfers ownership of a piece of your land. An easement leaves you the owner but gives someone the right to use a strip of it — and the terms can bind your land for decades.

Fee taking

They acquire that portion outright. You're paid for the land taken and for any damage to the rest of the property.

Easement

You keep title, but you accept restrictions: what they can build, who can enter, what you can no longer do over or near the strip, and sometimes the right to expand later. You're still owed compensation, both for the easement and for the impact on the remainder.

Why the terms matter as much as the dollars

Width, access rights, restoration obligations, exclusivity, and future-expansion language all affect how much your land is worth and how you can use it. A slightly bigger check can cost you for years if the terms are broad. Read the terms as closely as the price.

What to do the day you get a notice

Don't sign anything yet. The first 48 hours are about protecting your position, not making decisions.

  • Keep every document — letter, offer, appraisal, maps, business cards — together in one folder.
  • Write down any deadlines exactly as stated, but don't assume the first deadline is final.
  • Photograph your property as it is now: access points, structures, fences, crops, and anything of value.
  • Don't give recorded statements or sign access or possession forms you don't fully understand.
  • Get an independent read on value before you respond.
  • Remember you can negotiate — the first number is a starting point.

Who to call

Talk to a right-of-way consultant or eminent-domain professional who represents property owners — not the agency. The earlier you get someone in your corner, the more room there is to build the case for full compensation.
