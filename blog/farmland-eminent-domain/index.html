Farmland Eminent Domain: What Agricultural Landowners Need to Know

Highways, pipelines, and transmission lines are taking more farmland than ever. The compensation owed to farmers goes well beyond the value of the strip taken — and government appraisals routinely miss most of it.

National ROW › Guides › Farmland eminent domain

Why farmland condemnation is different

Farmland has characteristics that make eminent domain compensation more complex than a simple commercial or residential taking. Agricultural land value depends on soil type, drainage, irrigation infrastructure, field efficiency, and lease income potential — factors that a general appraisal may not adequately capture. And because agricultural operations are integrated across the whole farm, a taking that removes only a narrow strip can have disproportionate effects on the value and productivity of the remaining land.

Government appraisers are often generalists who value rural land by comparing sales prices per acre. That approach misses the operational economics of farming and systematically undervalues agricultural takings. What compensation farmland owners are entitled to

Fair market value of the land taken. The starting point is the per-acre market value of the strip or parcel acquired, based on comparable sales of agricultural land in the area. If your farmland has development potential or mineral value, that potential must be considered in the highest and best use analysis.

Crop and yield losses. If the taking occurs while crops are growing on the acquired strip, those crops are separately compensable. Additionally, soil disruption from pipeline installation, highway grading, or construction activity reduces crop yields on the disturbed area for years after construction — often five to ten years before soil productivity returns to pre-taking levels. Agricultural economists can document and value this yield reduction as a separate element of compensation.

Tile drainage system damage. Many Midwest and Great Plains farms have extensive tile drainage systems — networks of perforated pipe that manage soil moisture and enable high-yield row crop production. Pipeline and highway projects that cross farms routinely damage or interrupt tile systems. The cost to repair, reroute, or replace damaged tile drainage is a compensable impact that most agency appraisals do not adequately address.

Irrigation infrastructure. If the taking disrupts irrigation pivots, mainlines, risers, or water delivery systems, the cost to redesign and reconstruct the irrigation system is compensable. A center pivot that no longer clears the right-of-way boundary may require relocation or replacement — a cost that can be substantial and that should be documented before negotiation.

Field efficiency and operational impacts. A pipeline or highway crossing the middle of a field changes how it can be farmed. Reduced field efficiency — longer equipment passes, increased turning, inability to use full-width equipment — has measurable economic value over a farming operation’s life. These operational impacts compound over decades and should be quantified and included in the compensation claim.

Severance and remainder damages. When a taking physically separates a farm into multiple pieces, each piece may be worth less than its proportional share of the original whole. A landlocked remainder, a parcel cut off from irrigation infrastructure, or a field reduced below the minimum efficient operating size all suffer severance damage that is compensable beyond the land value of the strip taken.

Access impacts. Highway and transit projects sometimes eliminate or restrict access roads that farmers use to move equipment between fields. If the project closes a field access road or requires significantly longer routes to reach parts of the farm, those access impacts are compensable.

Trees and timber. Orchards, timber stands, and woodlots on the taken strip are separately compensable. The value of mature trees — particularly bearing orchards — can be substantial and requires specialized appraisal expertise.

What most farmers don't know about their rights

Many agricultural landowners accept condemnation offers without realizing they have the right to an independent appraisal, the right to negotiate, and — if the agency refuses a fair offer — the right to contest the compensation amount in court. The agency’s offer is a starting position, not a legal requirement. You do not have to accept it.

You also do not have to stop the project in order to get more money. Challenging the compensation amount is entirely separate from challenging the agency’s right to take the land. Most agricultural landowners who get professional representation end up receiving significantly more than the initial offer — often for items the agency appraisal did not even mention.
