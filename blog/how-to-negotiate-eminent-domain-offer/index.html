How to Negotiate an Eminent Domain Offer

You received an offer for your land. You do not have to accept it. Here is a practical guide to negotiating — and what the process actually looks like when done right.

National ROW › Guides › How to negotiate an eminent domain offer

The most important thing to know first

Negotiating an eminent domain offer will not stop the project. The government, utility, or pipeline company almost certainly has legal authority to proceed. What you can negotiate — and what you are legally entitled to — is the amount you get paid. The Fifth Amendment requires that government takings pay just compensation. That standard is not defined by whatever number the agency wrote on their first offer letter.

Challenging the offer is not being difficult. It is exercising a constitutional right.

Step-by-step: how to negotiate an eminent domain offer

  1. Do not sign or cash any checks yet — Cashing the agency’s check or signing the offer documents can be treated as acceptance of their terms in some jurisdictions. Read everything before you do anything. If you need time, ask for it in writing.
  2. Get a copy of the agency’s appraisal — In most states, you are legally entitled to receive the appraisal the agency used to calculate their offer. Request it immediately. This document shows you exactly what they valued, what assumptions they used, and — critically — what they did not include.
  3. Review the appraisal for what is missing — Agency appraisals commonly omit or undervalue: damages to the land you keep (remainder damages), access impacts, severance effects, crop and agricultural losses, and business impacts. Make a list of every element of your loss that is not reflected in their number.
  4. Get an independent appraisal — Hire a qualified, independent MAI-certified appraiser who works for you — not the agency. Their job is to document the true market value of what is taken and the full scope of damages. This appraisal becomes the foundation of your counter-position.
  5. Submit a formal counter-offer in writing — Based on your independent appraisal, submit a written counter-offer to the agency’s right-of-way department. Include documentation supporting each component of your claim. A written counter creates a record and signals that you are serious about pursuing full compensation.
  6. Negotiate directly with the agency — Most condemnation cases are resolved through negotiation before any formal legal proceeding. The agency’s right-of-way agents have authority to increase their offer, especially when presented with a well-documented independent appraisal. Many owners receive significantly more than the initial offer through negotiation alone.
  7. If negotiation stalls, escalate to formal proceedings — If the agency will not move to a fair number, property owners can challenge the compensation amount through a formal condemnation proceeding in court, where a judge or jury determines just compensation. This is not a lawsuit to stop the project — it is a proceeding to set the right price. Agencies often settle before trial rather than risk a higher jury award.

What makes a strong negotiating position

The strength of your negotiating position depends almost entirely on documentation. Agencies move when they see a credible independent appraisal, a documented damage claim they cannot easily dismiss, and a property owner who is clearly prepared to pursue formal proceedings if necessary. Vague objections without documentation almost never move offers. Specific, documented claims almost always do.

Common mistakes owners make when negotiating eminent domain offers

  • Signing too quickly — Agency timelines create urgency. The real deadlines are usually longer than they suggest.
  • Negotiating on emotion, not facts — Fair compensation is a legal and appraisal question. Anger about the project does not increase your settlement; documented damages do.
  • Accepting partial payments without reserving rights — Some agencies offer partial payments during negotiation. Accepting these without a written reservation of rights can limit your ability to claim more later.
  • Focusing only on the land value — The most commonly missed money is in remainder damages, access impacts, and operational losses — not in arguing about the per-acre price of the strip itself.
  • Waiting too long to get help — Deadlines matter. Some states have strict timelines for challenging compensation. The earlier you engage professional representation, the more options you have.
