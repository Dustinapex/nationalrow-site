What Is Just Compensation in Eminent Domain?

The Fifth Amendment says the government must pay just compensation when it takes private property. But what that standard actually requires is much broader than most owners realize.

National ROW › Guides › What is just compensation?

The constitutional requirement

The Takings Clause of the Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution states: "nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation." Every state constitution contains a similar provision. This guarantee applies to all forms of government and utility takings — highway projects, pipeline condemnations, transit acquisitions, utility easements, and any other exercise of eminent domain power.

Just compensation is not a favor. It is a constitutional right. And it is not defined by whatever number the agency puts in their first offer letter.

The legal definition of just compensation

Courts have established that just compensation generally means the fair market value of the property taken — defined as the price a willing buyer would pay a willing seller, with neither under compulsion and both having reasonable knowledge of the relevant facts. This standard is simple in concept but complex in application, because:

  • Fair market value must be based on the property’s highest and best use, not just its current use
  • The valuation date is typically the date of the taking, which can affect value significantly in changing markets
  • The condemned party cannot receive more than fair market value, but is entitled to no less
  • The agency’s own appraisal, while required in most jurisdictions, does not define the legal minimum — it is simply their estimate of fair market value

Just compensation includes more than the land taken

This is where most property owners are surprised. Just compensation is not limited to the strip or parcel the agency acquires. In a partial taking — where the agency takes part of your land but you keep the rest — just compensation also includes:

  • Severance damages — the reduction in value of the remaining property caused by being separated from the taken portion
  • Consequential damages to the remainder — reduction in value to the land you keep caused by the project itself (not just the taking) — proximity to a highway, access changes, noise, drainage impacts
  • Loss of access — if the taking changes or eliminates access points to your remaining property, that impact has measurable economic value
  • Damage to improvements — if structures, irrigation systems, fencing, or other improvements on the taken strip must be removed or relocated, those costs are compensable
  • Agricultural and crop losses — in many jurisdictions, growing crops and timber on the taken portion are separately compensable

What just compensation does NOT include under federal law

Federal just compensation law (which applies to federally funded projects) does not include compensation for business losses, good will, or consequential economic damages to businesses operating on the taken property — unless those items are included under state law. This is a significant limitation that varies by state. Some states are more generous than federal law in what they compensate; others are not. Knowing your state’s specific rules is essential to building a complete claim.

Why agency appraisals routinely understate just compensation

Agency appraisals are prepared by appraisers hired and paid by the agency. Those appraisers are professionals and are not typically instructed to shortchange owners — but the appraisal process is inherently shaped by the agency’s perspective. Remainder damages are often minimized. Highest and best use analyses sometimes understate potential. Access impacts are easy to overlook. Agricultural losses require specialized expertise that general appraisers may not apply.

An independent appraisal prepared by a qualified appraiser working for the owner — with no financial relationship to the agency — almost always identifies compensable items the agency’s appraisal missed or undervalued.

How to challenge an inadequate offer

When you believe the agency’s offer does not represent just compensation, you have options. In most states, you can submit written objections, request a review, provide your own independent appraisal, and negotiate directly with the agency’s right-of-way team. If negotiation fails, you can contest the compensation amount in a formal condemnation proceeding — a legal process where just compensation is determined by a judge or jury, not the agency.

Challenging the amount does not stop the project. It changes what you are paid for your share of it.
