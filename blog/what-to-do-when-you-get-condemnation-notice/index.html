What to Do When You Get a Condemnation Notice

Do not sign anything yet. The first 48 hours are about protecting your position, not making decisions. Here is what every property owner should do immediately.

National ROW › Guides › What to do when you get a notice

Step 1: Do not sign anything

The first thing any representative will ask you to do is sign. Do not — not the offer letter, not a right-of-entry, not an easement document. Once you sign, your negotiating position weakens and some options may close.

Step 2: Get an independent read

Talk to a right-of-way consultant or eminent domain professional who represents property owners, not the agency side. You are entitled to have your own expert review the valuation and the scope of the take.

Step 3: Gather your documents

  • The notice and any accompanying appraisal or offer letter
  • Your property deed and any existing survey
  • Photos of the property, access points, and improvements
  • Lease agreements if the property is income-producing

Step 4: Note the deadlines

Condemnation processes have hard deadlines that vary by state. Note every deadline in the notice and put them in your calendar. If you are not sure what they mean, ask before they pass.
