Easement vs. Fee Taking: What Is the Difference?

A fee taking transfers ownership of your land. An easement leaves you the owner but restricts what you can do. Both are compensable — and the terms matter as much as the dollars.

National ROW › Guides › Easement vs. fee taking

Fee taking

A fee taking transfers ownership of a piece of your land outright. The agency acquires that portion permanently. You are paid for the land taken and for any damage to the rest of your property.

Easement

An easement grants a utility, pipeline, or government agency the right to use a strip of your land for a specific purpose. You keep title, but you accept restrictions on what you can do on that strip.

Why the terms matter as much as the dollars

Width, access rights, restoration obligations, exclusivity, and future-expansion language all affect what you can do with your remaining land for decades. A poorly negotiated easement can reduce the marketability of your entire property long after construction is done.

  • Width and location of the permanent easement corridor
  • Temporary construction easement terms
  • Restoration obligations after construction
  • Future expansion clauses
  • Exclusivity — can you cross, farm, or build over it?
