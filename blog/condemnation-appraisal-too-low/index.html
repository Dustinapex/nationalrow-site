Condemnation Appraisal Too Low? Here's What to Do

The government's appraisal is not the final word on what your property is worth. If the number seems too low, it very likely is — and you have clear legal options to challenge it.

National ROW › Guides › Condemnation Appraisal Too Low

When a government agency or utility company condemns your property and presents you with an appraisal, that document can feel authoritative and final. It's professionally formatted, full of data, and backed by a licensed appraiser. But in eminent domain cases, the government's appraisal is almost always just a starting point — and property owners who accept it without question often leave substantial money on the table.

This guide explains why condemnation appraisals come in low, what your options are when they do, and how to pursue the compensation you're actually owed.

Why Government Appraisals Often Undervalue Property

Condemnation appraisals are conducted by appraisers hired by the condemning authority — the agency, utility, or pipeline company acquiring your property. While appraisers are required to follow professional standards, the methodology, assumptions, and comparable sales they use can significantly affect the result. Common reasons initial appraisals come in below fair market value:

  • Severance damages are understated or omitted: When only part of your property is taken, the reduction in value to the remaining land is often the largest element of just compensation. Government appraisals frequently minimize this component.
  • The "highest and best use" analysis is incorrect: Appraisers are supposed to value your property at its highest and best use — the most profitable legal use of the land. If the appraiser uses a lower-value use scenario, the entire appraisal will be deflated.
  • Comparable sales don't match your property: Appraisers select comparable sales from the market to establish value. If the comparables don't reflect your property's specific location, access, or characteristics, the value conclusion will be wrong.
  • Infrastructure damage is underestimated: The cost to repair or replace drainage systems, irrigation infrastructure, fencing, wells, or other improvements destroyed by the project is often treated as a minor adjustment rather than full replacement cost.
  • Easement percentage methodology: For easement takings, appraisers often apply a standard percentage of fee value rather than analyzing the specific impact of the easement on your property. That percentage may not capture the full damage.

You Are Entitled to See the Appraisal

Before responding to any offer, you have the right to obtain the condemning authority's appraisal. Under federal law (the Uniform Relocation and Real Property Acquisition Policies Act) for federally assisted projects, agencies must provide you with a copy of the appraisal that formed the basis of their offer. Under many state laws, similar rights exist regardless of federal funding.

When you receive the appraisal, review it carefully for:

  • The comparable sales used — do they actually reflect your property type and market area?
  • The highest and best use conclusion — does it match your property's actual potential?
  • Severance damage calculation — was it included? How was it calculated?
  • Infrastructure damage — what specific items were identified and how were they valued?
  • Date of value — is the appraisal current, or based on stale market data?

Get Your Own Independent Appraisal

The single most effective step you can take when you believe a condemnation appraisal is too low is to hire your own independent appraiser. An appraiser working for you has no interest in producing a low number — they're retained to find and document every legitimate dollar of compensation you're owed.

When selecting an independent appraiser:

  • Look for a Certified General Appraiser with experience in eminent domain and right-of-way work specifically
  • Ask about their experience with the type of property being taken (farmland, commercial, residential, pipeline easement)
  • Confirm they are familiar with your state's eminent domain laws and the specific project type involved
  • In some states, the condemning authority is required to reimburse your appraisal costs up to a statutory limit — ask about this before proceeding

Your independent appraisal then becomes the foundation for negotiation. Agencies that see a well-documented counter-appraisal from a qualified expert know they are dealing with a property owner who understands their rights and is prepared to fight for fair compensation.

Make a Documented Counter-Offer

Once you have an independent appraisal, submit a formal counter-offer to the condemning authority. This counter-offer should include:

  • Your independent appraisal report
  • A written summary of the specific elements where you believe the agency's appraisal is deficient
  • Documentation of any infrastructure damage, severance damages, or consequential losses not captured in the agency's appraisal
  • A clear statement of the total compensation you are seeking

Most eminent domain negotiations result in a settlement between the agency's initial offer and the property owner's counter-offer. A strong, well-documented counter-offer shifts the negotiating range substantially in your favor.

Administrative and Legal Options

If the agency refuses to move significantly from their initial appraisal, you have several escalation options:

Request an Administrative Review

Most agencies have an internal review process where you can request that a supervisor or chief appraiser review your documentation and reconsider the offer. This often produces better results than field-level negotiations, because decision-makers higher in the organization have more flexibility.

Demand Condemnation Proceedings

If negotiations fail, you can force the agency to file formal condemnation proceedings in court. This transfers the compensation decision to a judge and, in most states, a jury. Juries tend to be sympathetic to property owners and skeptical of government lowball offers — which is why the mere threat of a jury trial often motivates agencies to settle at a higher number.

Challenge the Taking Itself

If you believe the taking is for an illegitimate purpose, doesn't meet the legal requirements for public use, or the agency didn't follow the required acquisition procedures, you may be able to challenge the taking entirely, not just the compensation amount.

How Long Do You Have?

Deadlines in eminent domain vary significantly by state and by the type of project. Some agencies impose soft deadlines that can be extended with a phone call. Others have hard statutory deadlines. If you're unsure how much time you have, do not delay consulting with a right-of-way professional. Acting quickly preserves all of your options; waiting can foreclose some of them.

Common Mistakes Property Owners Make

  • Accepting the first offer without getting an independent appraisal: This is the most expensive mistake property owners make in eminent domain cases
  • Signing the deed or easement before negotiating: Once you execute the documents and accept payment, your right to seek additional compensation is typically extinguished
  • Negotiating without documentation: "I think my property is worth more" is not a persuasive negotiating position. An independent appraisal is
  • Assuming the process is adversarial: Many agencies are willing to negotiate in good faith when presented with solid documentation — it saves them the cost and uncertainty of litigation
