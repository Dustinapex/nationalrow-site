Condemnation Appraisal Too Low? Here Is What to Do.

The agency sent you their appraisal and you know the number is wrong. You are right to question it. Here is a step-by-step guide to challenging a low condemnation appraisal and getting what you are actually owed.

National ROW › Guides › Condemnation appraisal too low

Why government condemnation appraisals are often too low

When a government agency, utility, or pipeline company takes your property, they are required to base their offer on an appraisal. That appraisal is conducted by an appraiser they hire and pay. The appraiser is a licensed professional and is not typically instructed to shortchange you — but several structural factors push these appraisals low:

  • Agency appraisers often use comparable sales that favor the agency’s position, while ignoring sales that would support a higher value
  • Remainder damages — the reduction in value to the land you keep — are systematically underestimated or omitted in agency appraisals
  • Highest and best use analyses sometimes reflect the property’s current use rather than its most valuable potential use
  • Specialized damage categories like agricultural losses, access impacts, and severance effects require expertise that general appraisers do not always apply correctly
  • Agencies are under budget and schedule pressure; a lower appraisal reduces project cost and speeds acquisition

Step 1: Request the full appraisal in writing

In most states, property owners have a legal right to receive the agency’s appraisal before being asked to decide whether to accept the offer. If you have not received it, request it in writing immediately. The appraisal document is the foundation of everything — you cannot meaningfully challenge a number you have not seen the basis for.

Step 2: Review the appraisal for common errors

Once you have the appraisal, look for these common problems:

  • Missing comparable sales — Did the appraiser use the most recent and most similar sales? Are there higher sales they did not include?
  • Incorrect property description — Are the acreage, improvements, and land characteristics accurately described?
  • No remainder damage analysis — If you are keeping part of your property, does the appraisal value the damage to what you keep, or only the strip taken?
  • Wrong highest and best use — Agricultural land with development potential should be valued with that potential in mind
  • Missing items — Are fencing, irrigation systems, outbuildings, crops, or timber on the taken strip addressed?

Step 3: Get an independent appraisal

An independent appraisal is the single most important step you can take to challenge a low offer. You need an MAI-certified appraiser who works exclusively for property owners — not one who also does work for the condemning agency — and who has experience with the specific type of property and taking involved.

The independent appraisal should address every element of just compensation: the value of the land and improvements taken, remainder damages, agricultural losses, access impacts, and any other compensable elements the agency’s appraisal missed or undervalued.

Step 4: Submit a written counter-position

With an independent appraisal in hand, you or your representative submits a formal written counter-position to the agency’s right-of-way or acquisition department. This document presents your appraiser’s conclusions, identifies the specific errors or omissions in the agency’s appraisal, and states the compensation you believe is justified. A written counter creates a record, demonstrates seriousness, and often moves agencies that would not respond to verbal objections.

Step 5: Negotiate — then escalate if necessary

Most condemnation disputes settle through negotiation. Agency right-of-way staff have authority to adjust offers when presented with credible independent evidence. The goal of negotiation is a settlement that reflects fair market value and all compensable damages — not necessarily the highest possible number, but a defensible and complete one.

If the agency refuses to move to a fair number, the next step is a formal condemnation proceeding — a legal process where just compensation is determined by a court, not the agency. Many cases that seemed stuck in negotiation settle quickly once formal proceedings are filed, because agencies prefer to avoid unpredictable jury verdicts.
