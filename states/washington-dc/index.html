DC Eminent Domain Law Overview

Washington DC operates under a unique legal framework. Local condemnation proceedings are governed primarily by DC Code Title 16, Chapter 13 (§§ 16-1311 through 16-1322), while federal agencies operating within the District frequently proceed under federal law, including the Declaration of Taking Act (40 U.S.C. § 3114). Property owners in DC therefore face the possibility of either local or federal condemnation — and sometimes both simultaneously on the same project.

The DC Superior Court has jurisdiction over local condemnation cases. A jury may be empaneled under DC Code § 16-1315 to determine just compensation when the parties cannot agree. DC's courts have consistently interpreted just compensation to include the fair market value of the property taken, severance damages to any remaining parcel, and loss of access or visibility where appropriate.

Washington DC also enforces the protections of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments to the US Constitution, which are directly applicable to the District as a federal territory, requiring that no private property be taken for public use without just compensation.

DDOT and Local Agency Condemnations

The DC Department of Transportation (DDOT) acquires rights-of-way for roadway expansions, streetscapes, bicycle infrastructure, and multimodal projects throughout the District. DDOT follows its Right of Way Acquisition Manual and is required to comply with the federal Uniform Relocation Assistance and Real Property Acquisition Policies Act of 1970 (Uniform Act, 42 U.S.C. § 4601 et seq.) when federal funds are involved — which covers most major DDOT projects.

When DDOT cannot reach a negotiated settlement, it files a condemnation petition in DC Superior Court. Property owners may object to the taking, challenge the adequacy of compensation offered, and ultimately have the compensation amount determined by a jury.

WMATA and Transit Condemnations

The Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority (WMATA) is a bi-state compact agency created under DC Code Title 9, Chapter 11 and parallel legislation in Maryland and Virginia. WMATA has broad condemnation authority for rail, bus, and related transit facilities. WMATA takings may proceed under DC Code Title 16 for DC properties, with compensation disputes resolved in DC Superior Court.

DC Water Utility Easements

DC Water and Sewer Authority (DC Water), established under DC Code § 34-2202.01, has authority to acquire easements and fee interests for water mains, sewer lines, and green infrastructure improvements. DC Water must comply with the Uniform Act when acquiring private property and is required to provide property owners with written offers, appraisal reports, and opportunity to negotiate before initiating condemnation.

Federal Agency Takings in DC

Because Washington DC is the nation's capital, federal agencies including the General Services Administration (GSA), National Park Service (NPS), and the US Army Corps of Engineers also exercise eminent domain within the District. Federal takings proceed under the Declaration of Taking Act (40 U.S.C. § 3114), which allows "quick take" — the government deposits an estimated compensation amount with the US District Court for the District of Columbia and takes title immediately. Property owners can then litigate the adequacy of compensation in federal court while the project proceeds.

Quick Take Authority in DC

Both local DC agencies (when authorized by the DC Council) and federal agencies can utilize quick take procedures, allowing title transfer before the full compensation amount is determined. Under the Declaration of Taking Act, the government files a declaration and deposits estimated just compensation. Under DC Code § 16-1312, the Mayor may authorize immediate possession in certain infrastructure emergencies pending final compensation determination. In either case, property owners have the right to contest the deposited amount and pursue additional compensation.

Just Compensation in Washington DC

Under DC and federal law, just compensation in DC condemnation cases includes fair market value of the property taken — defined as the price a willing buyer would pay a willing seller in an arm's-length transaction. Where only a portion of a property is taken, the owner may also recover severance damages — the reduction in value of the remaining property caused by the taking and the proposed public use.

DC courts recognize that government appraisals frequently undervalue properties, particularly in a high-demand real estate market like Washington DC. Relocation Assistance Under the Uniform Act

Most federally funded DC condemnation projects — including those by DDOT, WMATA, and DC Water — are subject to the Uniform Relocation Assistance and Real Property Acquisition Policies Act (42 U.S.C. § 4601 et seq.). This entitles displaced residents and businesses to relocation payments, replacement housing payments, and reestablishment expenses for businesses of up to $50,000. Frequently Asked Questions — Washington DC Eminent Domain

What law governs eminent domain in Washington DC?

Local condemnation in Washington DC is primarily governed by DC Code Title 16, Chapter 13 (§§ 16-1311 through 16-1322). Federal agencies operating in the District — such as GSA, NPS, or the Army Corps — typically proceed under the Declaration of Taking Act (40 U.S.C. § 3114). Both frameworks require payment of just compensation.

Which agencies use eminent domain in DC?

The most active condemning authorities in Washington DC include DDOT for road and transit projects, WMATA for Metrorail and bus infrastructure, DC Water for utility easements, and various federal agencies including the GSA and National Park Service for federal facilities and parkland expansion.

Am I entitled to just compensation in DC?

Yes. DC Code § 16-1314 and the Fifth Amendment (directly applicable to DC as a federal territory) guarantee just compensation. This typically means fair market value for what is taken, plus severance damages for any reduction in value to your remaining property. Relocation assistance may also be available under the Uniform Act.

Can I challenge a DC condemnation taking?

Under DC Code § 16-1315, you have the right to a jury trial in DC Superior Court to determine the amount of just compensation. You may also challenge the condemning authority's legal right to take your property in certain circumstances. Consulting a qualified right-of-way professional early in the process is critical to protecting your rights.
