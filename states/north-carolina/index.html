North Carolina Eminent Domain Law

Eminent domain in North Carolina is primarily governed by North Carolina General Statutes Chapter 40A (Private Condemnors) and Chapter 136 (NCDOT). Any entity exercising eminent domain authority must comply with these statutes, which establish the procedures for filing, notice, deposit, and compensation determination. North Carolina uses a Quick Take procedure for NCDOT, meaning the state can take possession of the property before the final compensation amount is determined by a jury.

NCDOT Condemnation Under NCGS Chapter 136

The North Carolina Department of Transportation acquires right-of-way for state highways, secondary roads, and bridge replacements under NCGS 136-103 et seq. NCDOT prepares an appraisal of fair market value and any damages to the remainder of the property and makes a written offer. If the landowner rejects the offer or the parties cannot agree, NCDOT files a Declaration of Taking in superior court and deposits the appraised amount with the clerk. The landowner may withdraw the deposit without waiving the right to seek additional compensation. The jury determines the final compensation amount in a trial.

Private Condemnor Proceedings Under NCGS Chapter 40A

Private entities with eminent domain authority, including electric utilities, natural gas utilities, and some pipeline companies, must follow the procedures in NCGS Chapter 40A. The condemner files a complaint in superior court, and the clerk appoints three commissioners to assess compensation. Either party may appeal the commissioners' report to a superior court jury trial within thirty days. If the jury's award exceeds the condemner's final offer by fifteen percent or more, the court may award the owner reasonable costs and attorney fees.

What Just Compensation Covers in North Carolina

Under North Carolina law, just compensation includes the fair market value of the property actually taken, plus any reduction in the fair market value of the remaining property caused by the taking and the project. Damages to the remainder may include loss of access, drainage changes, proximity of the improvement, and loss of trees or improvements within the easement area. National ROW reviews NCDOT and private condemnor appraisals to identify elements of value that are commonly missed or undervalued.

Solar Farm and Wind Energy Easements in North Carolina

North Carolina has one of the fastest-growing solar energy markets in the country. Most solar developers are private companies that do not hold eminent domain authority and must negotiate voluntary leases or easements with landowners. National ROW reviews solar and wind lease proposals to ensure that per-acre payments, escalation clauses, decommissioning bonds, surface use restrictions, and restoration requirements adequately protect the landowner's long-term interests.

Transmission Line Easements in North Carolina

Electric utilities regulated by the North Carolina Utilities Commission may condemn easements for transmission lines under NCGS Chapter 40A. Compensation must reflect the market value of the easement taken and any severance damages to the remainder. Landowners are not required to accept the utility's initial appraisal and may contest the amount through the commissioner and jury trial process.

North Carolina Eminent Domain FAQ

What is the Quick Take process in North Carolina?

Under NCGS 136-103, NCDOT may file a Declaration of Taking and deposit its appraised value with the court. Title transfers to the state immediately, but the landowner has the right to contest the compensation amount. The landowner can withdraw the deposited funds while the case proceeds.

div>

How long does an NC condemnation case take?

NCDOT cases vary, but from filing to jury trial can take one to three years. Private condemnor cases under Chapter 40A often resolve at the commissioner stage or shortly after, but may also proceed to jury trial. National ROW helps landowners understand the timeline and prepare evidence at each stage.

div>

Can NCDOT take my entire property?

NCDOT typically acquires only the land needed for the project. However, if the remainder is left in a condition that substantially impairs its value or utility, you may have a claim for total taking or for substantial severance damages. An experienced right-of-way consultant can assess whether the remainder is viable.
