Florida Eminent Domain Law Overview

Eminent domain in Florida is governed primarily by Florida Statutes Chapter 73 (Eminent Domain) and Chapter 74 (Quick Taking). Any authority exercising the power of eminent domain must comply with these procedures. Common condemning entities in Florida include the Florida Department of Transportation, county expressway authorities, Florida Power and Light and other regulated electric utilities, natural gas utilities, and municipal water authorities.

Good Faith Written Offer Requirement

Florida Statutes Section 73.015 requires the condemning authority to make a bona fide written offer to the landowner before filing the condemnation petition or simultaneously with the petition. This offer must be based on an appraisal that reflects the property's full fair market value. The offer must also include a statement of the basis for the amount offered. Failure to make a proper good faith offer can result in dismissal of the condemnation petition.

FDOT Quick Take Procedure

Under Florida Statutes Section 74.051, FDOT and other authorized agencies may use the Quick Take procedure to obtain possession of property before the final compensation amount is determined by a court. The agency files a Declaration of Taking and deposits its estimate of compensation with the court. The landowner may then withdraw that deposit without waiving the right to seek a larger amount. FDOT's appraisals typically reflect the fair market value of the fee or easement taken, but often underestimate damages to the remainder, including loss of access, proximity impacts, and business damages in appropriate cases.

Just Compensation Under Florida Law

Florida Statutes Section 73.071 defines the compensation a landowner is entitled to receive. It includes the value of the property taken, any business damages to a business conducted on the remainder that are caused by the taking, and the reduction in value of the remaining property caused by the taking and the use of the condemned property. Florida's statutory right to business damages is broader than many other states and applies when a business operating on the remainder suffers losses directly caused by the road or project construction.

Attorney Fee Entitlement for Florida Landowners

Florida Statutes Section 73.092 provides that if the jury's verdict exceeds the condemner's last written offer by twenty-five percent or more, the landowner is entitled to recover reasonable attorney fees and costs from the condemner. This provision incentivizes condemning authorities to make fair offers and provides meaningful legal fee protection for landowners who succeed in challenging low offers.

Pipeline and Utility Easements in Florida

Florida regulated gas transmission companies and electric utilities have condemnation authority under Florida statutes. For voluntary solar and wind lease agreements, most developers do not hold condemnation authority and must negotiate with landowners. Florida Eminent Domain FAQ

Can I recover attorney fees in a Florida eminent domain case?

Yes, if the jury's verdict exceeds the condemner's final offer by 25% or more, you are entitled to reasonable attorney fees and expert costs under Florida Statutes 73.092. This is a significant protection for Florida landowners who take their cases to jury trial.

What is a business damage claim in Florida eminent domain?

Florida Statutes Section 73.071 allows certain businesses operating on the remainder of a condemned parcel to claim damages caused by the project. These business damages are separate from the property value claim and require evidence of actual business losses caused by the taking or the project's construction or operation.

How long does a Florida condemnation case take?

Florida condemnation cases vary by complexity and jurisdiction. Quick Take matters can put FDOT in possession quickly, but the compensation battle continues. Cases typically resolve within one to three years, depending on whether they reach jury trial.
