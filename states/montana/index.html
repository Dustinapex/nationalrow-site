Montana Eminent Domain Law Overview

Montana eminent domain law is governed by MCA Title 70, Chapter 30. Montana requires condemning authorities to make a bona fide offer based on an independent appraisal and to negotiate in good faith before filing a condemnation petition. Property owners are entitled to a jury trial on just compensation.

★ MCA §70-30-306: Good Faith Offer Requirement — Montana law requires the condemning authority to make a good-faith written offer based on an appraisal before filing for condemnation. If the final award substantially exceeds the offer, courts may consider attorney fee awards.

MDT Highway Condemnation

The Montana Department of Transportation acquires right-of-way for state highway projects, interchange expansions, and rural highway improvements statewide. I-90 corridor widening, US-2 improvements across northern Montana, and US-93 realignments regularly require right-of-way from private landowners and ranchers. Montana’s large ranch properties require specialized appraisal expertise to capture full agricultural value, grazing impacts, and water rights implications. National ROW represents Montana landowners in MDT proceedings, obtaining independent appraisals that account for ranch-specific values and all severance damages to the remainder.

Pipeline Easements in Montana

Montana sits at the intersection of major oil and gas pipeline networks serving the Bakken Formation in eastern Montana and North Dakota. TransWestern Pipeline, Kinder Morgan, and other midstream operators are active in the state. Pipeline easements in oil and gas producing areas command significantly higher per-rod values than agricultural areas — but initial offers frequently start well below market. National ROW negotiates pipeline easements for Montana landowners, ensuring full compensation for easement area value, surface damage, restoration requirements, and long-term operational restrictions.

NorthWestern Energy Transmission

NorthWestern Energy is the primary electric transmission provider in Montana and is actively expanding its grid to serve growing renewable energy development and meet WECC reliability requirements. Transmission line easements permanently restrict land use within the easement corridor, affecting grazing operations, irrigation system height, and future development potential. We negotiate transmission easements for Montana landowners, ensuring compensation reflects fair market value, agricultural impact, and the full scope of long-term use restrictions.

Wind & Solar Easements in Montana

Montana has significant wind energy development potential, particularly in eastern Montana, and is experiencing growing interest from solar developers. Energy developers make complex 30-to-40-year lease offers on wind turbine sites and solar farm easements. National ROW reviews wind and solar lease offers for Montana landowners, negotiating improved payment rates, escalation provisions, better surface use protections, and enforceable decommissioning requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions — Montana Eminent Domain

What law governs eminent domain in Montana?

Montana eminent domain is governed by MCA Title 70, Chapter 30. Condemning authorities must make a bona fide written offer based on an appraisal and negotiate in good faith before filing a condemnation petition. Property owners are entitled to a jury trial on just compensation.

What is just compensation in Montana?

Montana requires payment of fair market value for property taken, plus severance damages to the remainder. For ranch and agricultural properties, this includes the impact on grazing operations, water rights, irrigation, and the overall productivity of the remaining land.

Can pipeline companies use eminent domain in Montana?

Yes, pipeline companies that qualify as public utilities or common carriers under Montana law can exercise eminent domain authority. The specific authority depends on the type of pipeline and applicable state and federal law.

How are ranch properties appraised in Montana eminent domain?

Montana ranch properties require specialized appraisal expertise that accounts for grazing capacity, water rights, and agricultural productivity — not just bare land value. Agency appraisals frequently fail to capture full ranch value, making independent appraisal critical.
