California Eminent Domain Law Overview

Eminent domain in California is governed by the Eminent Domain Law, California Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) Sections 1230.010 through 1273.050. California has one of the most detailed and property-owner-friendly eminent domain frameworks in the United States.

Before filing a condemnation action, the condemning agency must adopt a resolution of necessity, conduct an appraisal, and make a written offer of just compensation to the property owner. The offer must be accompanied by a summary appraisal report under CCP Section 1255.010 et seq.

Caltrans and State DOT Condemnation

The California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) is the primary agency acquiring highway rights-of-way. Caltrans follows the Caltrans Right of Way Manual and is subject to the federal Uniform Relocation Assistance and Real Property Acquisition Policies Act as well as state law.

Under CCP Section 1255.010, Caltrans may seek prejudgment possession (quick take) by depositing its estimate of just compensation with the court. This allows construction to begin while the compensation dispute is litigated. Property owners can immediately withdraw the deposited amount without prejudicing their right to contest the valuation.

Pipeline and Utility Easements in California

Public utilities and pipeline operators in California must obtain easements through negotiation or condemnation. The California Public Utilities Commission regulates utilities, but the right to challenge easement compensation is preserved under the Eminent Domain Law.

Pipeline companies operating under state or federal authority may exercise eminent domain under California Public Utilities Code Section 1240 et seq. and CCP Section 1230.010. Landowners are entitled to just compensation including severance damages.

Just Compensation Under CCP Section 1263.310

Just compensation in California includes the fair market value of the property or rights taken, plus severance damages to the remainder if the partial taking reduces the value of what the owner retains. In cases involving business operations on the property, California also provides compensation for loss of goodwill under CCP Section 1263.510 — a benefit not available in most other states.

Attorney Fees and Litigation Expenses

Under CCP Section 1268.610, if the final judgment or verdict exceeds the agency's last written offer by the statutory threshold, the court shall award the property owner litigation expenses including attorney fees and expert witness costs. This provision incentivizes agencies to make fair initial offers and gives property owners leverage in negotiations.

Frequently Asked Questions

What law governs eminent domain in California?

California eminent domain is governed by CCP Sections 1230.010 through 1273.050. Agencies must adopt a resolution of necessity, make a written offer, and provide a summary appraisal before filing suit.

div>

Can Caltrans use quick-take procedures?

Yes. Under CCP Section 1255.010, Caltrans may seek prejudgment possession by depositing its estimate of just compensation with the court. Owners may immediately withdraw the deposit while continuing to challenge the amount.

div>

Does California compensate for business goodwill?

Yes. Under CCP Section 1263.510, California courts may award compensation for loss of goodwill to a business located on condemned property if the loss cannot be prevented by relocation. This is unique to California.

div>

Can I recover attorney fees in a California condemnation case?

Yes. Under CCP Section 1268.610, if the final award exceeds the last written offer by the statutory threshold, the court awards litigation expenses including attorney fees and expert costs.
