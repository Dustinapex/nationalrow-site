Georgia Eminent Domain Law Overview

Eminent domain in Georgia is governed by Georgia Official Code (OCGA) Title 22. State agencies use the procedures in OCGA Title 32 for highway right-of-way. Private entities with condemnation authority, including electric utilities, natural gas utilities, and some pipeline operators, follow the procedures in OCGA Chapter 22-1 and related provisions. Georgia also has a specific Quick Take statute that allows certain condemning authorities to acquire property before the final compensation is determined.

GDOT Highway Condemnation Under OCGA Title 32

The Georgia Department of Transportation acquires highway right-of-way under OCGA 32-3-1 et seq. GDOT prepares an appraisal and makes a written offer based on that appraisal. If the offer is rejected, GDOT files a condemnation petition and deposits its appraised value with the clerk of superior court. The landowner may immediately withdraw the deposit. The landowner then files a notice of appeal within thirty days to trigger a hearing before a special master or, if either party demands, a jury trial to determine the final compensation.

Special Master Proceedings in Georgia

Georgia's condemnation process uses a special master, an attorney appointed by the court, to receive evidence and determine just compensation in the first instance. Either party may appeal the special master's award to the superior court, where the case may be heard by a jury. If the ultimate award exceeds the condemner's original deposit by ten percent or more, the condemner must pay the landowner's reasonable litigation expenses, including attorney fees and expert costs, under OCGA 22-1-12.

Just Compensation in Georgia

Georgia law requires that just compensation equal the fair market value of the property taken, plus any consequential damages to the remainder of the parcel caused by the condemnation and the project. Consequential damages may include loss of access, drainage impacts, proximity of the project's infrastructure to remaining buildings, and severance of the property. Pipeline and Utility Easements in Georgia

Natural gas and petroleum pipeline companies with FERC certificates may exercise federal condemnation authority in Georgia. State-regulated gas and electric utilities have authority under OCGA Title 22. Voluntary solar and wind leases are negotiated agreements, as most solar developers do not hold condemnation authority in Georgia. National ROW reviews both voluntary easement offers and condemnation proceedings, negotiating improved compensation and better surface protection terms for Georgia landowners.

Timber and Agricultural Damages in Georgia

Georgia landowners may have claims for standing timber and agricultural crops destroyed or damaged by a condemnation or easement taking. These damage claims are in addition to the easement compensation itself. Timber value must be properly appraised to ensure it is included in the overall compensation package. Georgia Eminent Domain FAQ

Can I recover attorney fees in a Georgia condemnation?

Yes. Under OCGA 22-1-12, if the final award exceeds the condemner's original deposit by ten percent or more, the condemner is required to pay the owner's reasonable litigation expenses, which include attorney fees and expert witness costs. This is a significant incentive for condemning authorities to make fair initial offers.

What does the Georgia special master decide?

The special master hears evidence from both parties regarding the value of the property taken and any damages to the remainder. The special master issues an award that either party may appeal to the superior court for a de novo determination by a jury.

Can I withdraw the deposit in a Georgia condemnation?

Yes. You may withdraw the deposit with the clerk of court without waiving your right to seek additional compensation through the special master process or on appeal. Withdrawing the deposit does not constitute acceptance of the deposited amount as full compensation.
