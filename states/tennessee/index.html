Tennessee Eminent Domain Law Overview

Tennessee eminent domain is governed by T.C.A. Title 29, Chapter 16 (Sections 29-16-101 et seq.). Condemning authorities must attempt in good faith to negotiate a purchase before filing a condemnation petition in Circuit or Chancery Court. Upon filing, the court can authorize possession before the final compensation amount is determined. Landowners have the right to a jury trial on the amount of just compensation.

TDOT Highway Condemnation

The Tennessee Department of Transportation (TDOT) acquires right-of-way for Interstate and Tennessee highway improvements, bypass routes, and urban expressway projects. Active corridors include I-24, I-40, I-65, I-75, and the Nashville urban loop system. Tennessee’s rapid population growth in Middle Tennessee is driving significant new road capacity investments, with active condemnation activity in Davidson, Williamson, Rutherford, Wilson, and surrounding counties.

Pipeline Easements in Tennessee

Tennessee is crossed by major FERC-certificated natural gas pipelines including Tennessee Gas Pipeline (Kinder Morgan) and Columbia Gas Transmission. These interstate operators hold federal condemnation authority. Tennessee’s rapidly growing Nashville metro area is also driving new local natural gas distribution infrastructure investments. Landowners have the right to negotiate easement terms and contest inadequate compensation under T.C.A. Title 29 or federal law.

TVA & Local Power Company Transmission

TVA operates one of the largest power systems in the United States, serving most of Tennessee through a network of transmission lines and substations. TVA delivers power to 153 local power companies (LPCs) that distribute electricity to consumers. TVA holds federal condemnation authority under 16 U.S.C. Solar & Wind Easements

Tennessee has significant solar development activity, driven in part by TVA’s renewable energy procurement programs and private solar development in Middle and West Tennessee. TVA’s solar feed-in tariff and renewable energy contract programs have brought large utility-scale projects online across the state. The transmission infrastructure connecting these projects to TVA’s system can involve TVA’s federal condemnation authority.

Data Center Right-of-Way & Power Infrastructure

Middle Tennessee — particularly the Nashville metro and communities including Murfreesboro, Lebanon, Smyrna, LaVergne, and Mount Juliet — is one of the fastest-growing data center markets in the Southeast United States. Major technology companies, financial institutions, and hyperscale operators have invested heavily in the region, attracted by TVA’s low-cost power, available land, and disaster-resistant geography. TVA and its local power company partners are constructing significant new transmission and substation infrastructure to serve this demand, with active ROW acquisition programs affecting landowners in multiple Middle Tennessee counties.

Frequently Asked Questions — Tennessee Eminent Domain

What law governs eminent domain in Tennessee?

Tennessee eminent domain is governed by T.C.A. Title 29, Chapter 16 (Sections 29-16-101 et seq.). Condemning authorities must attempt to negotiate in good faith before filing a petition. Landowners have the right to a jury trial on just compensation.

What is TVA’s role in Tennessee condemnation?

TVA holds federal condemnation authority under 16 U.S.C. Â§ 831c and can condemn land for transmission lines, substations, and generation. TVA operates under federal law, but Tennessee courts have recognized T.C.A. Title 29 principles in TVA compensation proceedings. Landowners in TVA corridors need representation experienced with both TVA’s procedures and state law.

Does Tennessee have data center condemnation activity?

Yes. TVA is actively constructing new 500 kV and 230 kV transmission infrastructure in Middle Tennessee to serve hyperscale data center demand in the Nashville metro corridor. These ROW acquisitions follow T.C.A. Title 29 and TVA’s federal procedures, and landowners have full rights to just compensation.

What is just compensation in Tennessee?

Under Tennessee law and TVA’s federal framework, just compensation is the fair market value of the property taken plus severance damages to the remainder. Tennessee courts recognize loss of access, drainage impacts, and other consequential damages as compensable elements.
