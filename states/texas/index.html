Texas Eminent Domain Law — What Every Landowner Must Know

Texas is the most active state in the country for infrastructure condemnation. TxDOT highway expansions, cross-state natural gas and oil pipelines, CREZ electric transmission lines, solar farms, wind projects, and now a booming wave of data center power infrastructure are all driving right-of-way acquisitions across the state. Texas Property Code Chapter 21 gives landowners powerful rights — but only if you know how to use them.

★ Texas Landowner Bill of Rights (Texas Property Code § 21.0112)

Every entity with condemnation authority must provide you with a copy of the Landowner Bill of Rights before or when making an initial offer. This document outlines your right to a written appraisal, the right to reject the offer, the right to hire your own appraiser, and the right to a hearing before special commissioners and a full jury trial. If you were not given this document, the condemnation proceedings may be subject to challenge.

The Bona Fide Offer Requirement

Under Texas Property Code, the condemning authority must make a bona fide offer — one based on an actual appraisal — before filing a condemnation petition. They must provide you a copy of their appraisal report. If the offer is not based on a genuine appraisal or the appraisal is not delivered, the condemnation petition can be dismissed in district court. This procedural protection is unique to Texas and is one of the most powerful tools available to landowners.

TxDOT Highway Condemnation

The Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT) operates 25 district offices statewide — from the Bryan, Austin, and San Antonio districts to Houston, Dallas, Fort Worth, El Paso, Lubbock, Amarillo, and beyond. TxDOT acquires right-of-way for state highways, frontage roads, loop expansions, and US highway corridors that cut through farms, ranches, commercial properties, and residential land.

TxDOT appraisals consistently undervalue properties by omitting consequential damages to the remainder, failing to account for loss of access or visibility, and selecting comparable sales that don’t reflect highest and best use. National ROW reviews TxDOT appraisals and retains certified general appraisers to prepare counter-appraisals used in negotiations or at the special commissioner hearing.

Pipeline Easements in Texas

Texas has more pipeline mileage than any other state — over 450,000 miles of transmission and gathering lines crossing every region. Companies transporting natural gas, crude oil, and refined products may hold eminent domain authority as common carriers under the Texas Natural Resources Code, regulated by the Texas Railroad Commission (TRC). Before condemning, a pipeline company must demonstrate common carrier status and make a bona fide offer.

Landowners have the right to negotiate easement width, depth requirements for agricultural land, surface use restrictions, restoration obligations, crop and timber damage payments, and advance notice before operations disturb the surface. The initial offer from a pipeline company almost always understates the full impact on your land — including severance to crops, pasture, fencing, irrigation, and drainage.

Electric Transmission Line Easements

Electric utilities regulated by the Public Utility Commission of Texas (PUC) and competitive transmission developers authorized under the CREZ (Competitive Renewable Energy Zone) program hold eminent domain authority for high-voltage transmission infrastructure. ERCOT grid expansion is adding thousands of miles of new 345 kV and 500 kV lines across Texas to serve growing demand — particularly from data centers, industrial facilities, and the booming West Texas wind and solar regions.

Transmission line easement compensation must reflect the fair market value of the easement strip, damages to the remainder, impact on access, drainage, productivity, and any restrictions on land use under the easement corridor. Tower placement, clearing requirements, and height restrictions can permanently reduce your property’s value and utility.

Solar & Wind Easements in Texas

Texas leads the nation in both installed wind capacity and utility-scale solar development. Panhandle wind farms, Permian Basin solar projects, and South Texas renewable installations are all driving easement acquisitions from landowners across millions of acres. While most solar and wind agreements are voluntary easements, the power infrastructure that serves these projects — substations, collection lines, and transmission tie-ins — can involve entities with eminent domain authority.

Wind and solar easement agreements often run 30–50 years and contain terms that permanently restrict land use. Before signing any renewable energy agreement, have it reviewed by a right-of-way professional who understands the long-term value implications and what better terms look like.

Data Center Right-of-Way & Power Infrastructure in Texas

Texas has become one of the top data center markets in the United States, with major hyperscale campuses concentrated in the Dallas-Fort Worth Metroplex (Allen, Garland, Irving, Midlothian), San Antonio, Austin, and emerging markets in West Texas near renewable power sources. Data centers require massive amounts of electricity — often 100 to 500+ megawatts per campus — which means new high-voltage transmission lines, substations, and distribution infrastructure must be built to serve them.

The utilities and transmission companies constructing this power infrastructure — Oncor, AEP Texas, CenterPoint, and independent transmission developers — hold eminent domain authority and are actively acquiring rights-of-way across Texas to support data center load growth. Landowners in the path of these power corridors have the same rights as any other condemnation case: the right to a bona fide offer, the right to a counter-appraisal, and the right to a jury trial if compensation is disputed.

County & Local Condemners in Texas

Beyond TxDOT and private utilities, Texas landowners may face condemnation from county governments, municipal utility districts (MUDs), water supply corporations, flood control districts (including Harris County Flood Control District), river authorities, toll road authorities (NTTA, CTRMA, HCTRA), and municipal governments. Each entity has its own condemnation procedures, but all are subject to the bona fide offer requirement and just compensation standards of Texas Property Code Chapter 21.

Special Commissioner Hearings in Texas

When negotiations fail, the condemning authority files a condemnation petition in Texas district court. The district judge appoints three disinterested real property owners as special commissioners. Both parties present evidence of value, and the commissioners issue an award. Either party may object and appeal the award to district court, where the case is tried de novo before a jury. Frequently Asked Questions — Texas Eminent Domain

What is the Texas Landowner Bill of Rights?

Under Texas Property Code § 21.0112, every entity with condemnation authority must give you a copy of the Landowner Bill of Rights before or when making an initial offer. It outlines your right to a written appraisal, the right to reject the offer, the right to hire your own appraiser, and the right to a special commissioner hearing and jury trial.

How long do I have to respond to a TxDOT offer in Texas?

TxDOT must give you a reasonable period — typically at least 30 days — before filing a condemnation petition. Acting promptly allows your representative to review the appraisal, identify undervalued items, and request a counter before proceedings begin.

Do pipeline companies have eminent domain authority in Texas?

Yes, if they qualify as a common carrier under the Texas Natural Resources Code and are regulated by the Texas Railroad Commission. They must make a bona fide offer before condemning. Landowners can challenge both the authority to condemn and the amount of compensation offered.

Do data center developers have eminent domain in Texas?

Data centers themselves generally do not. However, the utilities building power infrastructure to serve them — Oncor, AEP Texas, CenterPoint, and transmission developers — do hold condemnation authority. If a new transmission line or substation is being built near your property to serve a data center project, you have full rights under Texas Property Code Chapter 21.

What is just compensation under Texas law?

Under Texas Property Code § 21.042, just compensation is the market value of the condemned property plus damages to the remainder caused by the taking and its use — including loss of access, severance, crop damage, fencing costs, and other consequential impacts.
