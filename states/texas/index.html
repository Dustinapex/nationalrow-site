Texas Eminent Domain Law: What Landowners Must Know

Texas has some of the most active condemnation markets in the country. TxDOT operates 25 districts covering more than 80,000 lane-miles. Pipeline companies, electric utilities, and now data center power developers are acquiring right-of-way at a pace unseen in decades. If you have received an offer or a knock at the door, you need to understand your rights immediately.

Texas eminent domain authority is governed by Texas Property Code Chapter 21. Condemning entities — including TxDOT, pipeline companies, electric co-ops, and investor-owned utilities — must follow a strict process: they must provide the Landowner Bill of Rights (Â§21.0112), obtain a written appraisal, and make a bona fide offer before filing a condemnation petition.

★ Texas Landowner Bill of Rights (Â§21.0112)

Under Texas law, every entity with condemnation authority must deliver the Landowner Bill of Rights to you before making an initial offer. It outlines your right to a written appraisal, your right to reject the offer, your right to hire your own appraiser or consultant, and your right to a special commissioner hearing and jury trial. Do not waive these rights without a professional review of the offer.

Special Commissioner Hearings

If you and the condemning entity cannot agree on compensation, the case goes to a panel of three special commissioners appointed by a county court judge. The commissioners hear evidence and set the award. Either side can then appeal to the district court for a full jury trial under Â§21.018. National ROW prepares landowners for every stage of this process.

Bona Fide Offer Requirement

The condemning entity must make a bona fide offer — a good-faith offer based on a written appraisal — before it can file a condemnation petition. If the offer is not bona fide, the entire proceeding can be challenged. National ROW reviews every offer for compliance before advising clients on next steps.

What Is the ERCOT Long-Range Transmission Plan?

ERCOT's Long-Range Transmission Plan (LRTP) is a series of major transmission projects designed to move power from generation-rich West Texas and the Panhandle to load centers in DFW, Houston, San Antonio, and Austin. The 765kV voltage class — higher than any existing Texas transmission lines — is needed to carry enormous amounts of power efficiently over long distances. Projects span multiple transmission owners including AEP Texas and Oncor.

These corridors run through West Texas, the Permian Basin, Central Texas, South Texas, and the Gulf Coast region. Affected counties include — but are not limited to — counties along the I-20 and I-10 corridors, the Hill Country, and rural North and South Texas. This project is expected to span 5 to 10 years of active construction and ROW acquisition.

What Landowners Along 765kV Corridors Need to Know

Transmission companies in Texas have eminent domain authority under the Public Utility Regulatory Act (PURA) and the Texas Utilities Code. If you and the utility cannot agree on compensation, they can condemn the easement through the special commissioner process under Texas Property Code Chapter 21. However, the company must make a bona fide offer first — and that offer must be based on a legitimate appraisal that accounts for all damages, not just the raw easement strip value.

What TxDOT Must Pay You

TxDOT is required to pay the market value of the property taken plus any severance damages to the remainder. This includes loss of frontage, loss of access, changes in drainage, impacts to irrigation, crop damage during construction, and any diminution in value to the rest of your tract caused by the project or its use.

Pipeline Easements in Texas

Texas is home to more pipeline infrastructure than any other state. Natural gas, crude oil, refined products, CO₂, and water transmission pipelines crisscross the state, and new pipeline projects are constantly being proposed. Major Texas pipeline operators include Kinder Morgan, Energy Transfer, Enterprise Products, TC Energy, Boardwalk Pipeline, and Enbridge.

Common carrier pipeline companies regulated by the Texas Railroad Commission (TRC) hold eminent domain authority under the Texas Natural Resources Code. However, the company must demonstrate valid common carrier status and make a bona fide offer before proceeding with condemnation. National ROW challenges lowball pipeline offers and ensures landowners are compensated for temporary workspace, permanent easement strips, damage to surface, crop damage, and long-term remainder impacts.

Solar & Wind Easements in Texas

Texas leads the nation in both wind and solar energy generation. The Permian Basin, West Texas, the Panhandle, and South Texas are home to some of the largest solar and wind projects in the country. Developers — including NextEra Energy, Invenergy, Vestas, Enel Green Power, Lightsource bp, and Pattern Energy — typically approach landowners with long-term easement or lease agreements lasting 25 to 40 years.

These agreements are complex, involve permanent or semi-permanent land restrictions, and require careful review. National ROW evaluates solar and wind agreements for fair annual payments, escalator clauses, decommissioning obligations, surface use restrictions, and impact on neighboring tracts.

Data Center Power Infrastructure & ROW — Texas

Texas — particularly the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex and its surrounding counties — has become one of the world's largest data center markets. Hyperscale campuses operated by Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon Web Services, and Oracle are driving demand for enormous amounts of new high-voltage power infrastructure. Every gigawatt of new data center load requires new transmission lines, substations, and distribution feeders — all of which require right-of-way from private landowners.

The 765kV ERCOT transmission expansion is directly connected to this data center power demand. Properties in Tarrant, Parker, Hood, Palo Pinto, Erath, Eastland, Callahan, Nolan, Taylor, and surrounding counties may be in the path of these new power corridors.

Data Center Developers Do Not Have Eminent Domain — But Their Utilities Do

Data center developers themselves (Microsoft, Google, Meta, etc.) do not have condemnation authority in Texas. However, the regulated electric utilities and transmission companies providing power to their campuses — AEP Texas, Oncor, and others — do hold full condemnation authority. If a utility contacts you about an easement tied to powering a data center, your rights are identical to any other transmission taking.

Frequently Asked Questions — Texas Eminent Domain

What is the ERCOT 765kV transmission project and how does it affect Texas landowners?

ERCOT's Long-Range Transmission Plan (LRTP) includes new 765kV extra-high-voltage transmission lines spanning hundreds of miles across Texas to move power from West Texas and the Panhandle to major load centers. AEP Texas and Oncor are the primary transmission developers. ROW acquisition is ongoing, and landowners in affected counties should not sign any easement without an independent review. The project spans multiple years and will require easements from thousands of private landowners.

What is the Texas Landowner Bill of Rights?

Under Texas Property Code Â§21.0112, every entity with condemnation authority must deliver the Landowner Bill of Rights to you before making an initial offer. It describes your right to a written appraisal, your right to reject the initial offer, your right to hire your own appraiser or consultant, and your right to a special commissioner hearing and jury trial. Failure to properly deliver this document can be challenged in the condemnation process.

How long do I have to respond to a TxDOT or utility offer?

TxDOT and utilities must give you a reasonable period to respond — typically at least 30 days per the bona fide offer letter. You are not required to accept the first offer. Acting promptly allows National ROW to review the appraisal, identify all compensable damages, and prepare a counter-offer before a condemnation petition is filed.

Do pipeline companies have eminent domain in Texas?

Yes. Common carrier pipeline companies regulated by the Texas Railroad Commission (TRC) have eminent domain authority under the Texas Natural Resources Code. However, the company must demonstrate valid common carrier status and make a bona fide offer before proceeding with condemnation. National ROW reviews whether the condemning entity's authority is properly established before advising on a response.

What is just compensation under Texas law?

Under Texas Property Code Â§21.042, just compensation is the market value of the property taken plus all damages to the remainder caused by the taking and its use. This includes loss of access, severance damages, crop damage, disruption to irrigation or surface operations, diminution of surrounding land value, and any other consequential impacts. National ROW works with qualified appraisers to document every element of compensable damage.

Do data center power lines give me the same rights as any other condemnation?

Yes. Landowners along transmission lines built to power data centers have the same rights as any other condemnation. The utility acquiring the easement must follow Texas Property Code Chapter 21, deliver the Landowner Bill of Rights, provide a written appraisal, and make a bona fide offer. National ROW has experience with these data center power corridor acquisitions in the DFW metro and surrounding counties.
