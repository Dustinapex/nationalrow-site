Texas Eminent Domain Law: What Landowners Need to Know

Texas is one of the most active states for infrastructure condemnation. TxDOT highway projects, cross-state pipelines, electric transmission lines, and solar and wind developments regularly require taking private land. The good news: Texas Property Code Chapter 21 gives landowners powerful procedural rights that, when exercised correctly, dramatically improve compensation outcomes.

The Texas Landowner Bill of Rights

Under Texas Property Code Section 21.0112, every entity with condemnation authority must provide landowners with a copy of the Landowner Bill of Rights before or when making an initial offer. This document outlines your core rights, including the right to receive a written appraisal, the right to reject the initial offer, the right to hire your own appraiser, and the right to a hearing before special commissioners and a jury trial if you disagree with their award.

The Bona Fide Offer Requirement

Texas law requires the condemning authority to make a bona fide offer before filing condemnation proceedings. A bona fide offer must be based on an actual appraisal, and the entity must provide you a copy of that appraisal. If the entity fails to make a proper bona fide offer, the condemnation petition can be dismissed in district court. This is a critical procedural protection unique to Texas.

TxDOT Highway Condemnation in Texas

The Texas Department of Transportation uses eminent domain to acquire land for state highways, frontage roads, and right-of-way expansions. TxDOT appraisals commonly undervalue properties by omitting consequential damages to the remainder, failing to account for loss of access or visibility, and relying on comparable sales that do not reflect the property's highest and best use. National ROW reviews TxDOT appraisals and, where warranted, retains a certified general appraiser to prepare a counter-appraisal used in negotiations or at the special commissioner hearing.

Pipeline Easements in Texas

Texas has more pipeline mileage than any other state. Companies transporting natural gas, crude oil, and refined products may hold eminent domain authority under the Texas Natural Resources Code. Before acquiring an easement by condemnation, the pipeline company must negotiate in good faith and make a bona fide offer. Landowners can negotiate the easement width, depth requirements for agricultural land, surface restoration obligations, crop and timber damage payments, and the right to receive notice before any operations disturb the surface. National ROW reviews pipeline easement offers and helps landowners obtain compensation that reflects the actual impact on their land.

Transmission Line Easements in Texas

Electric utilities regulated by the Public Utility Commission of Texas and competitive transmission developers authorized under the CREZ program have eminent domain authority. Easement compensation must reflect the fair market value of the easement taken, damages to the remainder parcel, and any impact on access, drainage, or agricultural productivity. Landowners are not required to accept the utility's initial appraisal and may challenge it through the special commissioner process.

Special Commissioner Hearings

If negotiations fail, the condemning authority files a condemnation petition in district court. The district judge appoints three disinterested real property owners as special commissioners. Both parties present evidence of value, and the commissioners issue an award. Either party may appeal the award to the district court, where the case is tried de novo before a jury. Texas Eminent Domain FAQ

How long do I have to respond to a TxDOT offer in Texas?

The condemning authority must give you at least the time specified in the written bona fide offer letter, typically 30 days, before filing a condemnation petition. Responding promptly is important so your representative can review the offer and request a copy of the appraisal.

Can I stop a condemnation in Texas?

Generally, you cannot permanently stop a condemning authority that has legal authority and a public purpose. However, you can challenge whether the authority has the legal right to condemn, whether the taking is for a valid public use, and whether proper procedures were followed. An attorney experienced in Texas condemnation law can advise on available challenges.

What is just compensation under Texas law?

Under Texas Property Code Section 21.042, just compensation is the market value of the property condemned plus any damages to the remainder of the tract caused by the condemnation and the manner of its use. This includes loss of access, severance damages, and other consequential impacts recognized under Texas law.
