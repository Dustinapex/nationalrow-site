Colorado Eminent Domain Law Overview

Eminent domain in Colorado is governed primarily by Colorado Revised Statutes Title 38, Article 1 (Sections 38-1-101 through 38-1-122). Any entity authorized by Colorado statute or federal law to exercise the power of eminent domain must comply with these procedures. Common condemning authorities in Colorado include the Colorado Department of Transportation, electric public utilities regulated by the Colorado Public Utilities Commission, natural gas pipeline companies, municipal utilities, and county road authorities.

CDOT Highway Condemnation in Colorado

The Colorado Department of Transportation acquires right-of-way for state highways and interstate projects under CRS 38-1-101 et seq. and CDOT's right-of-way manual procedures. CDOT prepares an independent appraisal of the property to be acquired and any damages to the remainder, then makes a written settlement offer. If the landowner rejects the offer, CDOT files a petition for condemnation in district court and deposits its appraised value. The court sets a hearing before commissioners or proceeds directly to jury trial, depending on the procedural posture of the case, to determine just compensation.

Just Compensation Under Colorado Law

Colorado law requires that just compensation be the fair market value of the property taken, plus any reduction in the fair market value of the remainder caused by the taking or the project. Colorado courts consider highest and best use of the condemned property, special adaptations of the property, and all legally cognizable damages to the remainder. National ROW evaluates CDOT and private condemnor appraisals to identify common valuation gaps, including undervalued severance damages, failure to account for loss of access or visibility, and errors in comparable sales selection.

Pipeline Easements in Colorado

Colorado has significant natural gas and oil pipeline infrastructure. Public utility pipelines may condemn easements under CRS Title 38. FERC-certificated interstate natural gas pipelines may use federal condemnation authority. For voluntary pipeline easement negotiations, landowners should ensure agreements address pipeline depth on agricultural land, restoration of soil profiles and fertility, tile drain repairs, crop damage payments, and the landowner's right to receive notice before any excavation on the surface. National ROW has experience representing Colorado landowners in both voluntary negotiations and condemnation proceedings.

Electric Transmission and Renewable Energy in Colorado

Colorado is a growing renewable energy state with significant wind, solar, and transmission development. Electric utilities regulated by the Colorado PUC may condemn easements for transmission lines under CRS Title 38. Most solar and wind developers do not hold condemnation authority and must negotiate voluntary easements. National ROW reviews both condemnation offers and voluntary renewable energy lease proposals to ensure Colorado landowners receive compensation that reflects the full value of the use of their land.

Oil and Gas Surface Use in Colorado

Colorado has a comprehensive regulatory framework for oil and gas operations affecting surface owners, including the Colorado Oil and Gas Conservation Commission. Surface use agreements govern the relationship between mineral operators and surface owners. National ROW helps surface owners in Colorado evaluate surface use agreement terms, negotiate improved damage compensation, and understand the relationship between surface use agreements and any right-of-way condemnation affecting their property.

Colorado Eminent Domain FAQ

Can I recover attorney fees in a Colorado eminent domain case?

Under CRS 38-1-122, if a condemnee recovers compensation exceeding the condemner's last written offer, the court may award litigation expenses including reasonable attorney fees. The specific circumstances and threshold for fee recovery should be reviewed with a Colorado attorney.

div>

How does the Colorado condemnation process work?

The condemner files a petition in district court and deposits its appraised value. The landowner may withdraw the deposit without waiving the right to seek more. The case proceeds to commissioners or jury trial where compensation is determined. Either party may appeal the commissioners' award.

div>

What is a surface use agreement in Colorado?

A surface use agreement is a contract between a mineral operator and a surface owner governing how oil and gas operations will be conducted on the surface. Under Colorado law, operators must negotiate in good faith with surface owners before drilling. National ROW reviews surface use agreements to ensure fair compensation and adequate surface protection provisions.
