Arizona Eminent Domain Law Overview

Eminent domain in Arizona is governed primarily by Arizona Revised Statutes Title 12, Chapter 11 (Sections 12-1111 through 12-1134). Any entity authorized by statute to exercise the power of eminent domain must follow these procedures. Common condemning authorities in Arizona include the Arizona Department of Transportation, utilities regulated by the Arizona Corporation Commission, municipalities, and county flood control districts.

The Condemnation Process Under ARS 12-1111

Arizona's condemnation process begins when the condemning authority files a complaint in superior court. The authority must first make a good faith written offer based on an independent appraisal of the property's fair market value. If the owner rejects the offer, the authority may file suit and deposit its appraised value with the court. The court then appoints a board of commissioners to determine just compensation. Either party may appeal the commissioners' award to a superior court jury trial within a specified time period.

ADOT Highway Condemnation in Arizona

The Arizona Department of Transportation regularly acquires right-of-way for state highways, interchange improvements, and frontage road projects. ADOT prepares appraisals and makes written offers to affected landowners. When ADOT's initial offer does not fully capture the value of the property taken and damages to the remainder, National ROW reviews the appraisal, identifies undervalued elements, and coordinates an independent appraisal when appropriate. Common issues include failure to account for severance damages, loss of access, changes in grade, and special damages to remainder improvements.

Pipeline and Utility Easements in Arizona

Natural gas transmission pipelines operating under Federal Energy Regulatory Commission certificates may exercise federal eminent domain authority in Arizona. State-regulated pipelines and electric utilities may exercise authority under Arizona statutes. For voluntary solar and wind leases where the developer does not hold condemnation power, Arizona landowners may negotiate lease rates, surface use restrictions, restoration requirements, and compensation for crop and livestock disruption. National ROW reviews both condemnation offers and voluntary easement agreements to ensure landowners receive appropriate compensation.

Just Compensation in Arizona

Arizona law requires just compensation equal to the fair market value of the property taken, plus any damages to the remainder of the parcel resulting from the taking or from the project itself. Under ARS 12-1122, the court appoints three commissioners who hear evidence and determine the amount of compensation. If either party appeals, the matter is tried before a superior court jury. Arizona also provides that if the jury's award exceeds the condemner's last written offer by more than 25 percent, the condemner must pay the owner's reasonable litigation expenses, including attorney fees and appraisal costs.

Immediate Possession in Arizona

Under ARS 12-1116, a condemning authority may apply to the court for immediate possession of property before the compensation amount is finally determined. The authority must deposit its appraised value with the court. The landowner may then withdraw those funds without prejudice to the right to seek greater compensation at trial. National ROW helps landowners understand what withdrawal of the deposit means for their ability to continue challenging the compensation amount.

Arizona Eminent Domain FAQ

Can I stop ADOT from taking my Arizona property?

If ADOT has legal authority and the project serves a public purpose, stopping the taking entirely is difficult. You can challenge whether proper procedures were followed and whether the authority has the right to condemn your specific parcel. An attorney licensed in Arizona can advise on available challenges. What you can always contest is the amount of compensation offered.

div>

How long does Arizona condemnation take?

Timeline varies by project and whether the case goes to commissioners or jury trial. From initial offer to resolution, Arizona condemnation cases commonly take six months to two years. National ROW helps landowners respond strategically at each stage to maximize outcomes.

div>

What if I withdraw the deposit in an Arizona condemnation?

Under ARS 12-1127, withdrawing the deposit does not prevent you from pursuing additional compensation. However, the procedures and any interest implications should be reviewed with legal counsel before withdrawing funds.
