New Mexico Eminent Domain Law Overview

New Mexico eminent domain law is governed by NMSA 1978, Sections 42A-1-1 through 42A-1-33. Condemning authorities must make a bona fide written offer before filing a condemnation petition. Property owners are entitled to a jury trial to determine just compensation. New Mexico is experiencing one of the largest infrastructure buildouts in its history, driven by the SunZia transmission project and rapidly expanding Permian Basin pipeline activity.

Frequently Asked Questions — New Mexico Eminent Domain

What law governs eminent domain in New Mexico?

New Mexico eminent domain is governed by NMSA 1978 §§42A-1-1 through 42A-1-33. Condemning authorities must make a bona fide written offer and negotiate in good faith before filing a condemnation petition. Property owners are entitled to a jury trial on just compensation.

Does SunZia have eminent domain authority in New Mexico?

Pattern Energy's SunZia project obtained a federal right-of-way across federal lands and has pursued negotiations with private landowners. Whether SunZia can exercise eminent domain authority over private land in New Mexico is a complex question that depends on the specific route segment and applicable law. Consult a representative before signing any SunZia easement.

What is just compensation in New Mexico?

New Mexico requires payment of fair market value for property taken, plus severance damages to the remainder. For ranch and agricultural properties, this includes the impact on grazing operations, water rights, access, and overall productivity of the remaining land.

How are Permian Basin pipeline easements valued in New Mexico?

Per-rod rates in active producing areas significantly exceed rates in agricultural areas — and initial offers from pipeline companies frequently start well below market. Independent appraisal is essential for any Permian Basin easement negotiation.
