The renewable energy land rush and what it means for landowners

The United States is building solar and wind capacity at an unprecedented pace. Utility-scale solar farms require hundreds or thousands of acres of flat, accessible land. Wind projects require easements for turbines, access roads, and interconnection lines across large swaths of agricultural land. The developers pursuing these projects are experienced, well-capitalized, and operating from lease templates their lawyers have spent years optimizing in the developer’s favor.

Most landowners are approached by a land agent who presents an offer as if it is standard and non-negotiable. It is not. Solar lease rates, wind easement payments, turbine setbacks, decommissioning requirements, and land use restrictions are all negotiable — and the difference between the first offer and a negotiated agreement can be substantial over a 30-year project life.

Solar farm leases — what to negotiate before you sign

Solar lease agreements are long, complex documents. The headline rate per acre per year matters, but it is only one of many issues that determine the real value of the deal over a 25- to 40-year term:

  • Per-acre annual rent — the base payment for land leased to the solar developer; should reflect comparable solar lease rates in your area, local land values, and proximity to transmission infrastructure
  • Escalator clause — does the rent increase with inflation (CPI) or a fixed percentage each year? A flat rate that looks good today will lose 40% of its real value over a 25-year lease without escalation
  • Lease term and extension options — many leases have an initial 25-year term plus multiple 5-year options the developer can exercise unilaterally; you should negotiate for co-equal renewal rights
  • Decommissioning and restoration — what happens when the solar panels reach end of life? The lease should require the developer to remove all equipment and restore the land at their cost, with a financial bond to guarantee it
  • Change of ownership provisions — solar projects are routinely sold to other developers or institutional investors; your rights should follow through any ownership change
  • Right to mortgage and assign — developers will pledge the lease as collateral for project financing; this needs careful limitation to protect your ownership rights
  • Exclusivity and interference restrictions — does the lease prevent you from installing your own solar panels, wind turbines, or other improvements on land you retain? These restrictions must be carefully bounded

Wind farm easements — key issues for landowners

Wind turbine easements are different from solar leases in several important ways. Each turbine requires a larger setback area and generates more significant visual, noise, and vibration impacts on surrounding land. The key issues to negotiate include:

  • Turbine payment per unit — payments are typically structured per turbine per year, often ranging from $5,000 to $20,000 or more depending on turbine size and regional norms
  • Shadow flicker and noise impacts — turbines can affect adjacent parcels and neighbors; the easement should specify setback distances and operational limits
  • Access road easements — wind projects require permanent roads to each turbine for maintenance; these are separate from the turbine easement and separately compensable
  • Interconnection and collection line easements — underground cables connecting turbines to the substation cross your land and restrict future use of those corridors
  • Agricultural use during the lease — most wind easements permit continued farming around the turbines, but the agreement should specify exactly what operations are permitted

Frequently asked questions — solar and wind easements

Is the solar developer’s standard lease agreement negotiable?

Yes. Developers present their standard form as a starting point, and experienced landowner representatives negotiate changes to it routinely. Rate, escalation, term length, decommissioning obligations, land use restrictions, and dozens of other provisions are negotiable. Do not sign the first version you receive.

What happens to my land if the solar project fails or the developer goes bankrupt?

This is one of the most important questions in any solar lease negotiation. The lease agreement should specify what happens to the equipment, who is responsible for removal costs, and what financial security (performance bond or letter of credit) backs up the decommissioning obligation. Without proper protections, you could end up with aging solar panels on your land and no solvent developer to remove them.

Can I still farm land that a solar developer is leasing?

For land covered by the solar arrays, typically no — the panels occupy the surface. Many solar developers now offer agrivoltaic arrangements where grazing or low-clearance crops are permitted under the panels, but this varies by project and must be specified in the lease. Land outside the solar array footprint can typically continue to be farmed.

What is a right of first refusal in a solar lease and should I agree to it?

A right of first refusal gives the solar developer the option to match any offer you receive if you try to sell your land during the lease term. This can significantly complicate any future sale of your property. Whether to accept this provision, and on what terms, is an important negotiating point that you should not concede without careful consideration.
