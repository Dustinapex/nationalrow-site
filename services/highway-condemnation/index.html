How highway condemnations work — and where owners get shortchanged

When a state Department of Transportation needs land for a new highway, interchange, road widening, or bypass, it has the legal authority to take it through eminent domain. The agency hires its own appraiser, makes an offer, and sends a notice that often feels more like a deadline than a starting point.

The problem is that DOT appraisals are built to move projects forward, not to maximize your payment. They frequently undervalue the land taken, ignore or minimize damages to the property you keep (called remainder damages), and miss compensation you’re entitled to for lost access, drainage impacts, crop losses, fence replacement, and business disruption.

What highway condemnation compensation should include

Just compensation in a highway taking is not just the strip of land the DOT marks on a plat. A full claim covers:

  • Fee value of the land taken — fair market value of the parcel or strip acquired, based on comparable sales not chosen by the agency
  • Remainder damages — reduction in value of the land you keep due to the project’s proximity, noise, drainage changes, or altered character
  • Severance damages — when the taking splits your property and leaves a less usable or landlocked remainder
  • Access impacts — if the highway closes, narrows, or limits driveways and access points to your property
  • Improvements taken — fences, buildings, irrigation systems, landscaping, or other structures on the acquired strip
  • Crop and timber losses — agricultural owners are owed compensation for crops, orchards, and timber on the taken portion
  • Business losses — in some states, business disruption during construction is compensable

Frequently asked questions — highway condemnation

Do I have to accept the DOT’s first offer?

No. In every state, the initial offer from a DOT is an opening position based on the agency’s own appraisal. You have the right to review that appraisal, hire your own appraiser, and negotiate for more. In most states, you also have the right to file objections and, if necessary, litigate the compensation amount in court — without stopping the project.

Can the DOT take my entire property even if they only need part of it?

Usually the DOT acquires only the strip or parcel needed for the road. But if a partial taking leaves a remainder so small or unusable that it has no independent value, you may be able to demand the agency acquire the whole property. This is called an inverse condemnation or an uneconomic remainder claim, and it varies by state law.

What is the difference between a fee taking and a temporary construction easement?

A fee taking is permanent — the DOT acquires full ownership of that strip of land. A temporary construction easement (TCE) allows the agency to use your land during construction and then return it. TCEs are compensable separately and are often undervalued in DOT offers. Both should be reviewed carefully.

How long does the process take?

Most highway condemnation negotiations resolve in three to nine months from our engagement. Cases that go to formal legal proceedings can take longer. We move quickly because DOT acquisition timelines are real and delays can hurt owners. Contact us as soon as you receive your notice.
