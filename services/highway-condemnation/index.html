// HIGHWAY TAKINGS

State DOTs make opening offers. We make sure yours isn’t your final answer.

When TxDOT, FDOT, Caltrans, NCDOT, or any other highway agency decides it needs your land, you receive a notice and an offer. That offer is built from their appraisal — designed to move the project forward, not to pay you the full amount you’re owed under the law.

You are entitled to just compensation — the fair market value of what’s taken plus the damage done to the land you keep. Most initial highway offers significantly undervalue remainder damages, access impacts, and frontage loss. A partial taking strip worth $40,000 can cause $200,000 in damage to the rest of your property.

Do not sign before you have an independent review. National ROW works exclusively on the property owner’s side — never the agency — on a contingency basis. If we don’t increase your payout, you pay nothing.

// FAQ

Highway & DOT Condemnation — Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to accept the highway agency's first offer?

No. The offer from TxDOT, FDOT, Caltrans, NCDOT, or any other highway agency is built from their own appraisal and is an opening position, not a final number. You have the right to an independent review and to negotiate before you sign anything.

What if only part of my property is being taken for the highway?

In a partial taking, you're owed for the strip of land taken plus the damage done to the remainder — including lost access, reduced frontage, and diminished use of the land you keep. That remainder damage is often far larger than the value of the strip itself and is commonly underestimated in the initial offer.

Do the same compensation rules apply to toll roads and county or city road projects, not just state DOTs?

Yes. Toll authorities, county governments, and municipalities follow the same just-compensation rules as state DOTs when they condemn property for a road project, so the same right to an independent review and negotiation applies.

What does it cost to have National ROW review my highway offer?

Nothing upfront. We work on contingency — if we don't increase your payout, you pay nothing.

How does an interchange or flyover project affect nearby properties differently than a simple road widening?

Interchange and flyover expansions often create severe remainder damage to nearby commercial and agricultural properties that goes well beyond the land directly taken, and these secondary impacts are frequently overlooked in the agency's initial offer.
