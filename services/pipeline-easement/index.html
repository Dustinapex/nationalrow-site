Why pipeline easement offers are almost always too low

When a pipeline company wants to cross your land, they send a land agent with an offer. That offer is built from the company’s internal formulas — a flat per-rod or per-acre rate that may not reflect actual market value, does not account for the specific characteristics of your property, and typically ignores the long-term restrictions the easement places on how you can use your land.

The company’s agents are experienced at getting landowners to sign quickly. The offer is designed to close fast. Our job is to make sure you understand what you’re signing away and that you’re paid fully before you do.

What full pipeline easement compensation should cover

A fair pipeline easement agreement compensates for every impact the line has on your property, not just the strip of land beneath it:

  • Permanent right-of-way strip — fair market value per rod or per acre for the land area subject to the easement, priced against comparable sales in your area
  • Temporary workspace easement — construction crews typically need additional land on each side of the pipeline trench during installation; this temporary use is separately compensable
  • Crop and yield lossesstrong> — agricultural landowners are entitled to compensation for crops destroyed or damaged during construction and for reduced yields in disturbed soil for years afterward
  • Soil disruption and drainage impacts — pipeline installation disrupts soil horizons, tile drainage systems, and subsurface structure; restoration is rarely complete
  • Restrictions on use — the easement agreement permanently limits what you can build, plant, or do in the right-of-way corridor; that restriction has real economic value the pipeline company does not offer you
  • Land splitting and field disruptionstrong> — a pipeline crossing the middle of a farm field changes how you can work it, sometimes forcing inefficient field patterns for decades
  • Proximity and stigma effects — high-pressure pipelines can reduce surrounding land values; these effects should be documented and claimed
  • Easement term and assignability — most pipeline easements are permanent and freely assignable to future companies; that scope must be reflected in the price

  • Hydrogen pipelines — early-stage projects requiring easements across agricultural land

Do pipeline companies have the right to take your land?

This depends on the type of pipeline and your state’s law. FERC-certificated interstate natural gas pipelines have federal eminent domain authority under the Natural Gas Act — meaning they can condemn your land in federal court if you refuse to grant an easement. In practice, this rarely happens because most negotiations settle before that point, and the threat of condemnation gives landowners the same right to just compensation that applies in highway and utility takings.

Gathering lines, oil pipelines, CO2 pipelines, and intrastate lines may or may not have condemnation authority, depending on state law. In Texas, for example, common carrier pipelines have limited condemnation rights under Chapter 111 of the Natural Resources Code. In other states the rules differ. Regardless of condemnation authority, you always have the right to negotiate the compensation amount — and you should before signing anything.

Frequently asked questions — pipeline easements

How is pipeline easement compensation calculated?

There is no single formula. Compensation should be based on the fair market value of the land affected by the easement (not the company’s internal rate sheet), plus documented damages including crop losses, soil impacts, use restrictions, and proximity effects. An independent MAI-certified appraisal specific to your land is the starting point for any serious negotiation.

div>

What is a reasonable per-rod payment for a pipeline easement?

Per-rod rates vary enormously by state, land type, and pipeline diameter. Agricultural land in the Midwest or Texas may see offers of $15 to $60 per rod, but fair market value for high-value agricultural or commercial land can be much higher. The only reliable way to know if the offer is reasonable is an independent appraisal — not the pipeline company’s rate card.

div>

Can I negotiate the easement terms, not just the payment?

Yes, and you should. Beyond the money, the easement agreement itself contains restrictions that affect your land for decades — what you can plant, build, or do in the right-of-way corridor; whether the company can assign the easement to other operators; how restoration is handled; and what happens if there is a leak or spill. These terms are negotiable before you sign.

div>

What if the pipeline company says the offer is non-negotiable?

That is a negotiating tactic. Pipeline companies negotiate easement terms and compensation with landowners regularly. If they claim the offer is final, that typically signals the opposite — they need your signature and expect pushback. Having professional representation changes the dynamic immediately.
