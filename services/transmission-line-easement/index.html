Why the transmission boom means more pressure on landowners right now

The United States is in the middle of a historic expansion of its electric transmission grid. New solar and wind generation requires massive investments in high-voltage transmission lines to move power from where it is generated to where it is used. Projects like the Permian Basin 765-kV lines in West Texas, multi-state grid interconnections, and regional reliability upgrades are crossing millions of acres of private land.

Every one of those crossings requires a permanent easement from the landowner. Utility companies send land agents with offers built from internal rate sheets — offers that do not account for the specific value of your land, the full impact of the easement on your remaining property, or the decades-long nature of the restrictions you are agreeing to.

What a transmission line easement compensation claim should include

  • Fair market value of the easement corridor — the per-acre value of the land burdened by the easement, based on comparable sales and the specific characteristics of your property — not the utility’s rate card
  • Tower and structure locations — transmission towers occupy land permanently; the area under and around each tower is often a separate compensable element beyond the corridor itself
  • Restriction on use value — within the easement corridor you cannot build, plant tall trees, or use the land in ways that interfere with the line; that restriction has permanent economic value
  • Remainder damages — the proximity of high-voltage transmission lines affects surrounding property values through visual impact, EMF concerns, agricultural limitations, and reduced market appeal
  • Agricultural and crop losses — construction disrupts farmland; access restrictions during maintenance affect operations long-term
  • Temporary workspace easements — construction requires additional land on each side of the permanent corridor; this temporary use is separately compensable
  • Access road easements — utilities often require permanent or semi-permanent access roads to tower locations; these are separate easements with separate values
  • Substation and switching station sites — if the project includes a substation on your property, this is typically a fee acquisition, not just an easement, and is valued differently

Types of transmission projects we handle

  • High-voltage direct current (HVDC) lines — long-distance transmission corridors crossing multiple states
  • 765-kV and 500-kV extra-high-voltage lines — the backbone of regional grid expansion, including projects in the Permian Basin and across the Sun Belt
  • 345-kV and 230-kV regional transmission lines — common in grid reliability and renewable interconnection projects
  • Distribution line upgrades and relocations — lower-voltage lines serving local areas, often relocated during highway or development projects
  • Substation site acquisitions — land acquisitions for new switching stations and substations in support of transmission projects
  • Fiber and telecom overlays — utilities frequently attach fiber optic cables to transmission towers; when this requires an expanded or new easement, owners are entitled to additional compensation

The Permian Basin 765-kV project — what it means for Texas landowners

Texas’s first 765-kV transmission lines, approved in 2025 to serve the Permian Basin, will cross hundreds of miles of West Texas ranchland, farmland, and mineral-producing acreage. These extra-high-voltage lines require wide corridors, tall steel lattice towers, and permanent easements that restrict land use for generations. National ROW is representing landowners along these corridors to ensure they receive full compensation — not just the per-acre rate the utility’s land agent walked in with.

The Duke Energy modernization project and similar utility upgrades in the Southeast and Midwest are creating similar easement acquisition waves. Wherever a new transmission line crosses private land, National ROW represents the owner.

Frequently asked questions — transmission line easements

What is a fair per-acre payment for a transmission line easement?

There is no universal answer. Fair compensation depends on your land’s market value, the voltage of the line, the width of the corridor, tower density, and the impact on the remainder of your property. Per-acre rates for agricultural land in the South and Midwest often range from $2,000 to $10,000 or more per acre in the easement corridor, but these numbers vary widely. Only an independent appraisal specific to your land will tell you if the offer is in the right range.

Do I have to allow a transmission line on my property?

Regulated electric utilities in most states have eminent domain authority to acquire transmission line easements. This means that if you refuse, the utility can file a condemnation action in state court to force the easement and have a judge or jury determine compensation. In practice, most cases settle in negotiation — but knowing that condemnation is on the table means you are entitled to just compensation, not whatever the land agent offers.

Can I still farm or graze under a transmission line easement?

Usually yes — most transmission line easements permit farming and grazing in the corridor, as long as crops or structures do not exceed a specified height. However, the restrictions on what you cannot do — no buildings, no tall trees, limited irrigation infrastructure in some cases — have real economic value that should be reflected in the compensation.

What happens if the utility wants to add more circuits or lines later?

This depends on the easement language. Some easement agreements grant the utility the right to add additional circuits or expand capacity in the future without additional compensation. This is a key negotiating point before you sign — the right to future expansion should either be excluded or separately priced in the easement agreement.
