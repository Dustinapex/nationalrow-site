Transit & Rail Condemnation

A transit agency is taking your property for a rail or bus project. You are entitled to full just compensation.

Light rail, commuter rail, and BRT projects require land from private owners along their corridors. Transit agencies make opening offers based on their own appraisals — and those offers routinely undercount what owners are owed.

How transit condemnations differ from highway takings

When a transit authority condemns property for a rail corridor, it is often acquiring land in dense urban or suburban areas where property values are higher and the impact on remaining parcels can be severe. A partial taking along a commercial corridor can eliminate parking, cut off access, reduce visibility, or physically divide a property in ways that substantially reduce its value — even if the strip taken appears small on a map.

Transit agencies are often under federal funding deadlines and project-opening pressures that motivate quick acquisition. Their land agents are experienced at moving through hundreds of parcels efficiently. Individual property owners are almost always at an informational disadvantage unless they have professional representation.

What a full transit condemnation claim should include

  • Fair market value of the property taken — for fee takings, the full market value of the land and improvements acquired, based on comparable sales and income approaches appropriate to the property type
  • Partial taking remainder damages — when only a strip or portion is taken, compensation for the reduction in value to what remains — including loss of parking, reduced frontage, access changes, and reconfiguration costs
  • Severance damages — when the taking physically splits a property, creating two parcels that are each less valuable than the original whole
  • Access impacts — if the rail corridor eliminates or significantly reduces driveway access, customer access, or loading dock access, these impacts are compensable
  • Construction impacts — temporary construction easements, noise, vibration, and traffic disruption during construction can be compensated in some jurisdictions
  • Business losses — some states allow compensation for business losses caused by the taking; this varies by state law and must be evaluated case by case
  • Relocation assistance — if you must relocate a business or residence because of the taking, federal Uniform Act relocation benefits apply on federally funded projects
  • Improvements on the taken area — buildings, site improvements, landscaping, and personal property that must be relocated

Transit and rail projects we work with

  • Light rail transit (LRT) — urban and suburban light rail corridors, including street-running and grade-separated sections
  • Commuter rail expansion — new station areas, corridor expansions, and double-tracking projects
  • Bus rapid transit (BRT) — dedicated busway corridors requiring permanent right-of-way acquisition
  • Heavy rail and subway expansions — underground and at-grade heavy rail projects in major urban markets
  • Freight rail corridor acquisitions — railroad expansion, siding, and yard development projects affecting adjacent private property
  • Transit-oriented development (TOD) acquisitions — land acquisitions by transit authorities for station-area development or park-and-ride facilities

The Purple Line — a National ROW transit project

The Purple Line light rail project in Maryland and the D.C. suburbs is a 16-mile corridor connecting communities in Prince George’s and Montgomery Counties. The project required forced takings from hundreds of property owners along its alignment — residential, commercial, and institutional parcels in dense suburban neighborhoods. National ROW represented owners along the corridor to secure compensation beyond the transit authority’s initial offers, navigating Maryland condemnation law and documenting remainder damages from this complex urban rail project.

Frequently asked questions — transit and rail condemnation

How quickly do I need to respond to a transit agency’s offer?

Faster than most people realize. Transit agencies operating under federal funding timelines may set short response windows, and some jurisdictions allow agencies to file for quick-take condemnation — taking possession before the compensation amount is even determined. Contact us immediately when you receive an offer or notice of intent to acquire. Delays can cost you negotiating leverage.

What is a quick-take condemnation?

A quick-take condemnation allows the agency to take immediate possession of your property before the compensation amount is finalized — typically by depositing their appraised value with the court. You can access those funds while the dispute over compensation continues. Quick-takes are common in transit projects with federal deadlines. If you receive a quick-take notice, get professional representation immediately.

The agency is only taking a small strip of my parking lot. Is it worth fighting?

Frequently yes. A strip of a parking lot that reduces your spaces below the minimum required for your business can affect your ability to operate, your lease terms, and your property’s market value — all of which are compensable beyond the strip’s land value alone. Small-looking partial takings in commercial areas often generate the largest discrepancies between the agency’s offer and full just compensation.

Can I recover legal fees in a transit condemnation?

In some states, property owners who successfully challenge a condemnation offer can recover attorney fees and expert costs if the final compensation award exceeds the agency’s initial offer by a sufficient margin. This varies by state law. On National ROW cases, we work on a contingency basis — you pay only if we win you more than the original offer.
