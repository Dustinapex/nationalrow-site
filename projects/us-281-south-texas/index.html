What TxDOT's Offer May Miss

South Texas rural and agricultural land along US 281 has unique characteristics that generic TxDOT appraisals may not fully capture.

Ranching & Agricultural Value

South Texas ranching operations, livestock grazing, and dryland farming have active income value that standard appraisals may underestimate.

Remainder Tract Damage

When TxDOT takes a strip from your ranch or farm, the remaining land may lose usability, access, or market value — all compensable.

Fence & Infrastructure Replacement

Miles of ranch fencing, cattle guards, water wells, and stock tanks are real assets that must be accurately valued and replaced.

Access & Operational Disruption

Loss of direct highway access, cattle crossing points, or equipment access routes can severely impact ranch operations and land value.

Mineral & Surface Rights

In South Texas oil and gas country, the interaction between surface takings and subsurface mineral rights deserves careful independent analysis.

Severance of Ranch Tracts

Highway expansion that splits a working ranch into non-contiguous pieces may eliminate operational viability — a significant compensable loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

TxDOT said their offer is fair — is that true?

TxDOT is required to make a good-faith offer, but "good faith" doesn't mean maximum value. Their appraisers work for TxDOT, not for you. An independent review often reveals additional compensable items.

Can I negotiate if I haven't signed yet?

Yes — and that's the best time to negotiate. Before you sign anything, you have maximum leverage. Get an independent review immediately.
